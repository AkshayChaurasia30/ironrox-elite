Pacing Strategy

The most common HYROX mistake is going out too fast on Run 1. Correct pacing is the single biggest determinant of your finish time — more so than raw fitness.

🏃 Runs 1–3 · Controlled Start
Effort: 6–7/10
10–15 sec/km slower than your solo 1km PB. Should feel conversational — if you can't speak, you're going too fast.
🏃 Runs 4–6 · Threshold Hold
Effort: 7–8/10
Hold your planned race pace exactly. Resist urges to surge. Station fatigue is now real — run "through" the discomfort without backing off.
🏃 Runs 7–8 · Empty the Tank
Effort: 9–10/10
If you paced correctly, Run 8 should be your fastest. Everything left in the tank goes here — arrive at Wall Balls with maximum remaining energy.

8 Mistakes That Cost Time

Every one of these has been learned painfully on race day by thousands of athletes. Know them before your first race and you'll have a massive advantage over the field.

❌
Going out too fast on Run 1
The #1 cause of blowups. Run 1 should feel embarrassingly easy. 10–15 sec/km slower than your solo PB.
❌
Only training running — ignoring stations
Running fitness alone won't save you at station 4. Every station must be trained at race weight, weekly.
❌
Failing reps — time penalties add up
Partial knee on lunges, short wall ball throws — judges are strict. Practise perfect reps at race pace.
❌
Under-fuelling mid-race
HYROX burns 900–1,400 kcal. No gels = glycogen crash in the final 3 stations. Fuel at km 3–4 minimum.
❌
Neglecting grip strength training
Sled Pull and Farmers Carry destroy weak grip. Add dead hangs and farmer walks every week. No chalk allowed.
❌
Skipping brick workouts
Running straight from a station is a skill. Practise run-to-station transitions every week or race day is brutal.
❌
Arriving cold — no warm-up
15 minutes minimum. A cold body hitting race pace on Run 1 is an injury in slow motion. Non-negotiable.
❌
Never simulating the full race
If you've never done the complete sequence in training, race day will be a brutal, expensive surprise. Simulate monthly.

🏃 The Running Segments
🏃 8 × 1km Runs
1km Running Segments
Total: 8km · Pace target: 4:15–4:35/km (Open) · Indoor track
Each run is 1km on an indoor track looping around the arena. You run 8 of them — one before each station. Running is the glue that holds HYROX together, and pacing it correctly is the single biggest determinant of your finish time.
Start controlled: Run 1 should feel embarrassingly easy. 10–15 sec/km slower than your solo 1km PB. You have 7 more ahead.
Cadence over stride: Aim for 175–180 steps/min. Short quick strides are more efficient than long bounding ones.
Breathing 2–2 pattern: Inhale for 2 steps, exhale for 2. Keeps your diaphragm relaxed and heart rate steady.
Arms drive pace: 90° elbow angle, drive straight forward-back, not across your body. Relaxed hands.

⚙️ The 8 Stations — Full Breakdown
⛷️ Station 1
SkiErg
Distance: 1,000m · Target: 3:20–3:50 (Open) · Machine: SkiErg Concept2
Simulate the double-pole skiing motion. Both arms drive overhead cables simultaneously downward in a powerful pull. One of the more aerobic stations — heart rate builds fast. Primarily targets lats, core, and shoulders.
Hinge at the hips: Drive arms all the way past your thighs — not just down to waist height. Full range = full power.
Rhythm first: Find a smooth 1-second pull cycle. Consistent strokes beat explosive bursts that fade.
Target pace ~3:30: Use the screen's split tracker. Anything under 4:00 for Open is solid.
LatsCoreShouldersTricepsHip Flexors
🛷 Station 2
Sled Push
Distance: 50m (25m + turn) · Men: 152kg · Women: 102kg (includes sled)
Drive a heavily loaded sled down 25m, turn, and return. One of the most quad-dominant and cardiovascularly brutal stations. The weight creates significant resistance even on smooth floors.
Stay low always: Think sprint-start position throughout. The moment you stand up, you lose push power dramatically.
Short fast steps: Tiny rapid strides beat long powerful pushes. Drive the floor away behind you.
Attack the turn: Lower your hips even more at the turn — this is where most athletes slow down.
QuadsGlutesCalvesHip FlexorsCore
🔗 Station 3
Sled Pull
Distance: 50m · Men: 152kg · Women: 102kg · Rope hand-over-hand
Face away from the sled, hold a rope, and hand-over-hand pull it toward you for 50m. Unlike the push, this heavily taxes biceps, upper back, and forearms. Grip endurance is the limiting factor for most athletes.
Lean back and use bodyweight: Don't just use your arms. Your torso lean provides mechanical advantage.
Train grip separately: Dead hangs, farmer walks, fat-grip pulls. Nothing is allowed in competition — no chalk, no straps.
Alternate hand depth: Take long pulls, not tiny grabs. Maximise each pull cycle.
BicepsLatsForearmsUpper BackCore
🦺 Station 4
Burpee Broad Jump
Distance: 80m total · Aim 8m per jump · Most aerobically demanding station
Perform a full burpee (chest to floor), then explode forward as far as possible in a broad jump. Immediately go into the next burpee from your landing position. Repeat for 80m total. At station 4, fatigue is accumulating — this station requires significant mental toughness.
8m per jump target: Mark it in training. Below 7m and you're losing significant time. Explode from the hips.
No full stand: Flow directly from landing into the next burpee. Standing up wastes 1–2 seconds per rep.
Breathe at the top: Take your inhale as your arms reach overhead before the jump. Never hold breath.
Full BodyQuadsChestShouldersAerobic System
🚣 Station 5
Row (Concept2 Ergometer)
Distance: 1,000m · Target pace: 1:55–2:05/500m (Open) · Damper: 4–6
1000m on the Concept2 rower. The drive sequence is always: Legs → Back → Arms. At race fatigue (station 5), technique breaks down fast — arms take over and efficiency collapses. Maintaining the sequence consciously is the challenge.
Sequence obsession: Legs drive first (60% power). Back opens second. Arms pull last. Never arms-first.
Controlled slide: Take a full 1–1.5 seconds to return to the catch. Rushing the recovery wastes energy and raises heart rate unnecessarily.
Damper 4–6: This is not a strength test. Lower damper = better aerobic efficiency. Higher only if you're very strong.
Legs (60%)BackArmsCoreAerobic
💼 Station 6
Farmers Carry
Distance: 200m (4 × 50m) · Men: 2×32kg · Women: 2×24kg kettlebells
Carry two kettlebells for 200m — four 50m laps. Looks deceptively simple but at station 6, grip strength and postural control are severely compromised. This station catches many athletes off guard late in the race.
Walk briskly, don't run: Running with KBs disrupts posture and wastes energy. Fast purposeful walking is faster overall.
Shoulders packed: Resist the urge to shrug. Pack shoulder blades down and back — prevents neck fatigue and injury.
Set down only at markers: At each 50m turn you can set the KBs down for a moment. Use it strategically, not every lap.
Forearms/GripTrapsCoreGlutesPosture
🎒 Station 7
Sandbag Lunges
Distance: 100m · Men: 30kg · Women: 20kg · Knee must fully contact floor each rep
Carry a sandbag on one shoulder and lunge 100m — four 25m lengths. The back knee must make full contact with the floor on every single rep or you receive a time penalty. Station 7 is a quad-destroyer at the worst possible moment in the race.
Full knee contact every rep: Judges watch for this. Don't hover — full touch. Practice this in training religiously.
Alternate shoulders at turns: Switch the sandbag at each 25m point to balance muscular load and prevent one-sided fatigue.
Chunk into 25m blocks: "Just reach the turn" is the only mental goal. Count to the turn, reset, repeat.
QuadsGlutesHip FlexorsCoreBalance
🏀 Station 8 — FINAL
Wall Balls
Reps: 100 · Men: 6kg ball / 9m target · Women: 4kg ball / 9m target · Final station
The grand finale — 100 consecutive medicine ball throws to a 9m target. You squat to parallel, then drive through your legs to launch the ball. At station 8, with 7km of running and 7 stations behind you, your legs will be devastated. Mental toughness determines everything here.
It's a leg drive, not a press: Your shoulders fatigue fast if you arm-press every rep. Generate all power from the squat — hips open, legs extend, arms just guide.
Sets of 20–25: Go unbroken in sets with a controlled 3-breath reset between sets. This is far faster than grinding to failure and collapsing.
Catch height = consistency: Aim to catch the ball at chest height every time. A consistent catch rhythm prevents the cognitive load of adjusting.
This is your finish line: Rep 100 ends your HYROX race. Every rep is one step closer. Count down from 50 once you're past halfway.
